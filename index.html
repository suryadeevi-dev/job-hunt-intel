Why two tools? Because the jobs are different.

Every "use AI for your job search" guide recommends one tool and tries to make it do everything. That's the wrong model. Researching 10 companies in parallel is a fundamentally different task than preparing deeply for a senior-level interview loop. Forcing one tool to do both means doing both badly.

Kimi K2.5's Agent Swarm mode is purpose-built for parallel breadth. It spins up to 100 specialized sub-agents working simultaneously, each independently searching and synthesizing. It's fast, inexpensive at API rates, and exceptionally good at decomposing large research tasks across many targets. But it has no persistent memory of who you are across sessions, and it's overkill for focused, iterative prep conversations.

Claude Projects gives you something different: persistent context. Your profile, resume bullets, impact stories, and known gaps live in the Project Instructions once — and every conversation in that project inherits them automatically. It's the right tool for nuanced, iterative, human-in-the-loop interview preparation. Running it sequentially across 10 companies for initial research, though, is slow and unnecessary.

"Use the swarm to find the targets worth preparing for. Use the persistent context to prepare well. Don't conflate them."

Mode 1 — Research

Kimi K2.5 Agent Swarm

Kimi K2.5 by Moonshot AI is currently the most capable open model for agentic batch work. Its Agent Swarm mode doesn't require you to define any workflow — the orchestrator decomposes your task autonomously and assigns specialized sub-agents to each piece. You write one prompt. It determines the parallelism.

Access

Go to kimi.com → click the mode switcher → select Agent Swarm (Beta). API access is available at platform.moonshot.ai with an OpenAI-compatible endpoint. Agent Swarm is in beta and available to paid users with free credits for initial exploration.

Prompt 1 — Parallel company intelligence

This is the foundational research prompt. Swap the bracketed company list for your own actual targets — the structure works the same whether you're researching hyperscalers, AI-infra startups, fintech companies, or enterprise software firms.

KIMI AGENT SWARM — company research 
# kimi.com → Agent Swarm mode

I am a [your role] with [X] years of experience.
Core stack: [your primary languages, platforms, and tools].
Target level: [Senior / Staff / Principal].

Task: For each company below, research IN PARALLEL and produce
a structured block per company covering:

1. Open [SRE / Platform / DevOps / your target role] roles
   — title, level, direct link if available
2. Known tech stack — infra, observability, CI/CD tools
3. Engineering blog highlights — reliability or infra challenges
   published in the last 6 months
4. Interview format for my target role — sourced from
   Glassdoor, Blind, or Levels.fyi
5. Compensation range at my target level — sourced from Levels.fyi
6. My fit score 1–10 with the single biggest gap to address

Target companies:
[Company 1], [Company 2], [Company 3], [Company 4], [Company 5],
[Company 6], [Company 7], [Company 8], [Company 9], [Company 10]

# End with a markdown table: one row per company,
# sorted by fit score descending. Flag top 3 to prioritize.
Fill in your role, stack, level, and company list before running. The swarm handles all 10 in parallel — expect results in ~20 minutes.

What you get back: a prioritized intelligence table that previously took an afternoon to assemble manually. The top 3 flagged companies become your deep-prep targets in Mode 2.

Kimi tip — be specific about output format. The swarm follows your instructions faithfully, including mediocre ones. Specify structure explicitly: "structured block per company," "markdown table at the end," "one section per JD." The more precise your prompt, the more copy-paste-ready your output. Vague instructions produce vague results.

Mode 2 — Prep

Claude Projects

Your top targets are identified. Now you need depth — not breadth. Claude Projects gives you a persistent context workspace where your complete professional profile exists once and every subsequent chat builds on it automatically. No re-pasting your resume. No re-explaining your background. Claude already knows you.

Go to claude.ai → Projects → New Project. Name it something like "Job Search 2026." In the Project Instructions field, paste your master context using the template below. Adapt every bracketed field to your own background — this is not a fill-in-one-size-fits-all template, it's a structure.

# When I have a gap, help me frame it honestly and proactively.
Replace every italic gold field with your own details. The quality of your prep output is directly proportional to how completely you fill this in.

How to build your impact story bank

The most valuable thing in your Project context is a well-structured set of impact stories. This is what Claude draws on when mapping stories to behavioral questions — and the specificity of the metrics is what separates a memorable answer from a generic one. Use this structure for each story:

IMPACT STORY TEMPLATE — fill one per major project or achievement
Story name
A short label you'll use to reference it — e.g., "DB migration," "Incident playbook," "Cost reduction Q3"
Context
What was the situation before you acted? What was broken, slow, risky, or missing? e.g., Legacy datastore causing p99 spikes under load, no alerting, on-call team flying blind
Action
What specifically did you do? Own your contribution clearly — avoid "we." e.g., Designed and led migration to managed cloud datastore, built observability layer, ran phased cutover
Result
The measurable outcome. This is the most important field. Quantify everything you can. e.g., p99 latency −60%, MTTR −89%, on-call pages −35%, became the migration playbook for 3 other teams
Learning
What would you do differently, or what did this teach you? Shows self-awareness and growth. e.g., Would have involved the on-call team earlier — they surfaced edge cases we only discovered mid-migration
Best fits
Which types of behavioral questions does this story answer best? e.g., "Tell me about a time you improved reliability," "Describe a complex technical project you led," "How do you handle ambiguous problems?"

Story coverage to aim for: Try to have at least one strong story per category — a reliability/uptime win, a latency or performance improvement, a process or tooling improvement, a cross-team or stakeholder influence story, and a mistake or failure story. Together they cover ~90% of behavioral questions at any senior level.

Your numbers don't need to be dramatic — a 30% improvement you can speak to confidently beats a 98% improvement you fumble explaining. Specificity and ownership matter more than scale.

How to identify and document your tooling gaps

Don't guess at your gaps — extract them from the data. Open 5–10 JDs from your target roles, list every tool or skill mentioned, and mark each as "have it," "adjacent to it," or "gap." The "adjacent" ones are your strongest gap-handling stories in interviews: you have a direct equivalent and a reason for the difference.

Framing gaps in interviews: A gap you've documented and thought through is a strength, not a weakness. "I use CDK rather than Terraform — the mental model is identical, and I've been building with it in my own side projects to close that gap" is a confident, prepared answer. An undocumented gap discovered live in an interview is a red flag. Claude will help you prepare the framing once you've listed them honestly.

Step 2: Pre-interview prep (one prompt, 48 hours out)

CLAUDE — pre-interview prep prompt 
I have a [phone screen / HM round / technical loop / final round]
at [Company] for [Role, Level] in [X days].

JD: [paste full job description]

Please:
1. Search their engineering blog for recent reliability or infra posts
   and summarize what problems they're publicly working on
2. Map my top 3 impact stories to this JD — for each, name the story,
   the specific metric to lead with, and why it fits this role
3. Generate 5 likely behavioral questions based on this JD and
   company culture, with my strongest story mapped to each
4. Generate 4 technical questions I should prepare for given their stack
5. Name my single biggest gap for this role and how I should
   frame it confidently in the interview
6. Draft 3 sharp, specific questions to ask the hiring manager
   that show I've done my research
After the initial brief, keep the conversation going. "Let's practice story #2 — push back on my answer." "That answer ran long, help me cut it to 90 seconds." Claude holds full context throughout.

Step 3: Iterate — this is the real value

The pre-interview prompt gives you the brief. The conversation that follows is where the real preparation happens. Because your full profile and the JD are both in context, you can practice naturally: ask Claude to challenge your answers, tighten your stories, or roleplay a skeptical interviewer. This is preparation that scales in quality the more you use it — and it all stays within one chat thread per company.

Layer 3 — ATS

The Submission Filter

Both modes produce strong content. Neither guarantees that content actually clears an ATS. That's what this layer is for. The rule is simple: nothing goes out below a 75% match score. Run every resume through an ATS scoring tool before submitting, every time.

$49/mo
Jobscan

Gold standard for ATS match scoring. Paste your resume and the JD, get a percentage score. Suggests exact missing keywords to add. Aim for 75%+. Use after Kimi tailors your bullets.

free tier
Teal

Best all-in-one if you want fewer tools: ATS resume builder, application pipeline tracker, LinkedIn optimizer. Free tier covers most active searchers. 4.9/5 rating on Chrome Web Store.

200 free/mo
Uppl.ai

200 free ATS scans per month plus 3 free tailored resumes. Best cost-to-value for high-volume searchers who want scan capacity before committing to a paid plan.

The three-step flow: Kimi generates tailored bullets → Jobscan or Uppl.ai scores them → if below 75%, take the specific low-scoring keywords back to Claude and ask it to adjust those bullets. Usually one pass is enough to clear the threshold.

The Weekly System: How the Two Modes Interlock

The two modes don't run at the same time — they hand off to each other. Kimi identifies and enriches your targets. Claude prepares you to win them. The ATS layer runs in between. Here's what a typical week looks like end to end:

Sunday
Run Kimi swarm across 10–15 targets. Get company intelligence table. Identify top 3–5 for the week.
~45 min
Monday
Run Kimi batch JD analysis. Check ATS scores with Jobscan. Hit 75%+ on each. Submit tailored applications.
~60 min
Tue–Thu
Run Claude pre-interview prep for each confirmed interview. Iterate on stories, practice Q&A, tighten gap framing.
~60 min/day
Fri
Warm outreach: 2–3 LinkedIn messages to people at target companies. Follow up on applications gone silent.
~30 min
Ongoing
Update your Teal pipeline. Note interview stages, pending decisions, recruiter responses. Prep Sunday's Kimi target list.
~15 min/day

A note on warm outreach: For senior roles, 70–80% of positions are filled via referral or warm intro before they're ever publicly posted. The AI stack handles the application pipeline — but a short, specific LinkedIn message to a current employee ("I noticed you work on the infrastructure team at [Company] — I'm exploring Staff SRE roles and would love to learn what the on-call culture is like") can move you from the ATS pile to the top of a recruiter's list. Dedicate Friday to this. It's not in any tool's scope.

Going Further: API-Based Automation

If you write Python regularly, you can hit the Kimi API (OpenAI-compatible) to build a lightweight batch tailoring script — feed it a folder of JDs and your base resume, get a tailored output per JD automatically. At standard API rates, running this across 20 JDs costs under a dollar.

If Python isn't your day-to-day: skip this section entirely — the manual Kimi workflow covered above gets you 90% of the value. This is purely an efficiency layer for engineers who want to automate the repetitive part.

Python — Kimi API batch tailoring script 
from openai import OpenAI
import json, concurrent.futures

# Kimi exposes an OpenAI-compatible API — swap your key and base_url
client = OpenAI(
    api_key="YOUR_MOONSHOT_KEY",
    base_url="https://api.moonshot.ai/v1",
)

def tailor_for_jd(base_resume: str, jd: str, company: str) -> dict:
    resp = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="kimi-k2-5",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": f"""
You are an elite tech resume strategist.

BASE RESUME:
{base_resume}

TARGET ROLE ({company}):
{jd}

Return JSON only — no preamble, no markdown fences:
{{
  "summary": "2-sentence tailored summary for this role",
  "top_bullets": ["bullet 1", "bullet 2", "bullet 3", "bullet 4"],
  "ats_keywords": ["kw1", "kw2", "kw3", "kw4", "kw5",
                   "kw6", "kw7", "kw8", "kw9", "kw10"],
  "best_story": "strongest impact story name for this role and why"
}}"""}],
        response_format={"type": "json_object"}
    )
    return json.loads(resp.choices[0].message.content)

with concurrent.futures.ThreadPoolExecutor(max_workers=5) as ex:
    futures = {ex.submit(tailor_for_jd, resume, jd, co): co

Feed the ats_keywords output directly into Jobscan to verify your ATS match score before submitting. The API is cloud-agnostic — the same script works regardless of whether your background is AWS, GCP, or Azure.

What Not To Do

Don't mass auto-apply. Tools that auto-submit 100+ applications daily produce generic, low-scoring submissions and risk getting your LinkedIn account flagged. Targeted volume — 10–15 well-tailored applications per week — consistently outperforms spray-and-pray. Quality over noise, every time.

Don't use Kimi for prep or Claude for batch research. The whole point of this system is that each tool does what it's built for. Running 10-company research sequentially in Claude is slow. Running iterative CARL story refinement in Kimi without persistent context is frustrating. Respect the mode boundaries.

Don't let AI write your metrics. AI generates strong structure, keyword alignment, and story framing. The specific numbers in your bullets — the percentage improvements, the timelines, the scale of impact — come from you. These are also what interviewers probe on. If you can't defend a metric in conversation, don't put it in your resume. Your numbers don't need to be dramatic. They need to be real and yours.

Don't skip warm outreach. No AI tool replaces a referral. A specific, thoughtful message to a current employee at your target company can leapfrog your application past the ATS entirely. Build it into your weekly routine — Friday is the suggested slot above. Ten minutes of warm outreach often outperforms an hour of additional resume tuning.

The Bottom Line

The tech job market in 2026 rewards systems, not effort hours. A well-structured AI campaign — Kimi finding and enriching your targets, Claude preparing you to win each interview, Jobscan making sure every submission clears the filter — compresses a full week of manual work into six or seven focused hours. And it works regardless of your specific target companies, cloud background, or role type.

Two modes. Three supporting tools. One system. Set it up once, run it weekly, and spend your actual energy on what only you can do: showing up to interviews with real stories, specific metrics, and genuine preparation.

To start this week: (1) Create a Claude Project and spend 30 minutes filling in your master context — especially the impact story bank. (2) Run one Kimi Agent Swarm research prompt across your current targets. (3) Run your resume against your top 3 JDs in Jobscan. You'll have more actionable intelligence after those three steps than most people accumulate in a week of searching.
